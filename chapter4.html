Chapter 4: Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL) in Series Circuits

4.1 What is Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL)?

Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL) states that the sum of all voltage drops around a closed loop equals the total applied voltage. KVL is essential for analyzing voltage distribution across each component, ensuring circuits function as expected, especially in complex systems where voltage must be balanced.

KVL Formula:

Vtotal = V1 + V2 + … + Vn

Example:
If a circuit has a voltage source of Vtotal = 12 V with components that drop voltages V1 = 4 V, V2 = 5 V, then:

Vtotal = V1 + V2 + V3 = 12 V

4.2 Applying KVL with Complex Impedance in AC Circuits

In AC circuits, KVL calculations use complex numbers to handle the magnitude and phase of impedance. This means that summing voltage drops involves both the real and imaginary components, allowing us to analyze not only how much voltage is dropped but also the phase shift in the circuit.

Example:
Given a circuit with Z1 = 3 + j4 Ω and Z2 = 5 + j2 Ω, and a current of I = 1 A:

  • Voltage drop across Z1: V1 = I ⋅ Z1 = 3 + j4
  • Voltage drop across Z2: V2 = I ⋅ Z2 = 5 + j2
  • Total Voltage: Vtotal = V1 + V2 = 8 + j6

4.3 Non-Ideal Voltage Sources and Internal Resistance

Real-world voltage sources often have internal resistance, impacting the voltage distribution across a circuit. Internal resistance is typically modeled in series with the source, and it influences both the total voltage drop and the current flow in the circuit, which can be crucial for ensuring accurate voltage delivery to load components.

Example:
For a voltage source Vs = 12 V with internal resistance Rs = 1 Ω and load RL = 4 Ω:

Effective voltage across RL: VRL = (Vs / (Rs + RL)) ⋅ RL

4.4 Step-by-Step KVL Analysis in Series AC Circuits

Using KVL in AC circuits requires the following steps:

  1. Identify Components: List all components in the loop and their impedance values.
  2. Calculate Individual Voltage Drops: Use V = IZ for each component, applying the current and each component's impedance.
  3. Sum Voltage Drops: Add up the real and imaginary parts of each voltage drop to get the total voltage.
  4. Compare with Source Voltage: Ensure the sum matches the source voltage to verify KVL is satisfied.

Example:
In a circuit with ZR = 4 Ω and ZL = j3 Ω, with a source voltage of Vtotal = 8 + j3 V:

  • Current I = 1 A
  • Voltage across ZR: VR = 4 V
  • Voltage across ZL: VL = j3 V
  • Sum Vtotal = VR + VL = 8 + j3

4.5 Analyzing Bridge Circuits with KVL

A bridge circuit provides a more complex application of KVL. By applying KVL to each loop in a bridge circuit, we can solve for unknown voltages or currents in various branches, which is useful in balancing voltages or analyzing sensor circuits in audio applications.

Example:
In a balanced bridge circuit with resistors in each arm, KVL helps verify the balance by equating voltage drops around each loop.

4.6 Phasor Diagrams and Voltage Summation

Phasor diagrams visually represent KVL in AC circuits by showing each voltage drop as a vector (phasor). This approach illustrates the summation of voltages as the vector sum of all voltage phasors in a loop, making it easier to understand complex AC relationships.

Example: In an R-L circuit with VR = 4 V and VL = j3 V, a phasor diagram displays the total voltage as the vector sum of VR and VL.

4.7 Applications of KVL in Audio Circuits

KVL is crucial in audio circuit design, especially for analyzing the frequency response of filters and amplifiers. Voltage drops across components like resistors, capacitors, and inductors directly impact how frequencies are filtered or amplified, influencing the overall sound quality.

Example: In an audio filter circuit, KVL helps calculate voltage drops across resistors and capacitors, shaping the output frequency response and ensuring desired sound characteristics.
