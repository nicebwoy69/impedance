3.1 Impedance in AC Circuits

In AC circuits, impedance combines resistance (R) and reactance (X) to represent the total opposition to current flow, encompassing both magnitude and phase. This complex representation is essential for analyzing how AC signals interact with resistors, capacitors, and inductors.

Formula: For a component with resistance R and reactance X,

Z = R + jX

where Z represents impedance as a complex number, R is the resistive part, and X is the reactive part.

Example:
In an R-L circuit with R = 4 Ω and XL = 3 Ω,

Z = 4 + j3 Ω

3.2 Impedance Calculations in Series Circuits

When components are in series, their impedances add directly. This is useful in calculating the total opposition to current flow in circuits with multiple components, like R-L-C circuits.

Series Impedance Formula: For impedances Z1 = R1 + jX1 and Z2 = R2 + jX2,

Ztotal = Z1 + Z2 = (R1 + R2) + j(X1 + X2)

Example:
For an R-L-C circuit with ZR = 2 Ω, ZL = j5 Ω, and ZC = -j3 Ω,

Ztotal = 2 + j(5 - 3) = 2 + j2 Ω

3.3 Calculating Voltage Drops and Phase Angle

Using Ohm’s Law with complex impedance, we calculate voltage drops across each component in an AC circuit:

V = IZ

where:

  • V is the voltage across the component,
  • I is the current,
  • Z is the impedance of the component.

The phase angle (θ) between voltage and current reveals how out of sync these two quantities are, which affects power transfer and efficiency in the circuit.

Example:
For current I = 2 A in an R-L circuit with Z = 4 + j3 Ω:

Voltage: V = I ⋅ Z = 2 ⋅ (4 + j3) = 8 + j6 V

Phase Angle: Calculate the phase angle θ between voltage and current using:

θ = tan-1 (X / R)

3.4 Power Calculations in AC Circuits

In AC circuits, power calculations involve three types of power:

  • Apparent Power (S): Total power in the circuit, combining real and reactive components, measured in VA.

    • S = |V| ⋅ |I|

  • Real Power (P): The actual power consumed or used in the circuit, measured in watts (W).

    • P = |V| ⋅ |I| ⋅ cos(θ)

  • Reactive Power (Q): Power stored and returned by reactive components, like capacitors and inductors, measured in VAR.

    • Q = |V| ⋅ |I| ⋅ sin(θ)

Example:
If |V| = 10 V, |I| = 2 A, and θ = 30°:

  • Apparent Power: S = 20 VA
  • Real Power: P = 20 ⋅ cos(30°) = 17.32 W
  • Reactive Power: Q = 20 ⋅ sin(30°) = 10 VAR

3.5 Power Factor and Its Importance

The power factor (PF) describes the ratio of real power to apparent power in an AC circuit, indicating the efficiency of power usage. A higher power factor implies less energy wasted in reactive components, which is especially valuable in audio circuits for clear, efficient sound amplification.

Formula:

Power Factor = cos(θ)

3.6 Resonance in Series RLC Circuits

In an RLC circuit, resonance occurs when the inductive reactance (XL) and capacitive reactance (XC) cancel each other, minimizing total impedance and maximizing current flow. Resonance is essential in audio circuits for tuning to specific frequencies, as seen in speakers and filters.

Resonant Frequency (f):

f = 1 / (2π√(LC))

Example: For L = 1 H and C = 1 μF,

f ≈ 159.15 Hz

3.8 Applications in Audio Circuits

Impedance, phase angle, and resonance are critical in audio circuits, like speaker crossovers and equalizers. Understanding how these elements work together helps in designing circuits that distribute frequencies effectively.

Example: In a speaker crossover, components are chosen to achieve specific impedance and phase relationships, helping divide frequencies to deliver the right sounds to different drivers.
